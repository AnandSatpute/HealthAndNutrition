    Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle / TCA Cycle)¶

    The Krebs cycle — also called the Citric Acid Cycle or Tricarboxylic Acid (TCA) Cycle — is the central metabolic hub of aerobic energy production. It occurs in the mitochondrial matrix and functions as the convergence point for carbohydrate, fat, and protein catabolism, feeding electrons into the electron transport chain (ETC) to generate ATP.

    🔬 Overview¶

    Property Detail
    Location Mitochondrial matrix
    Net inputs (per turn) 1 Acetyl-CoA, 3 NAD⁺, 1 FAD, 1 ADP/GDP, 1 H₂O
    Net outputs (per turn) 2 CO₂, 3 NADH, 1 FADH₂, 1 GTP (or ATP), 1 CoA-SH
    Turns per glucose 2 (one per pyruvate → acetyl-CoA)
    Total ATP per glucose (via ETC) ~20–24 ATP from NADH/FADH₂ generated by TCA
    Regulation type Substrate availability + allosteric + product inhibition

    ⚙️ The Pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA Bridge (Pyruvate Dehydrogenase Complex)¶

    Before entering the Krebs cycle, pyruvate (from glycolysis) must be converted to Acetyl-CoA. This irreversible step is performed by the Pyruvate Dehydrogenase Complex (PDC).

    Pyruvate + CoA + NAD⁺  ──[PDC]──►  Acetyl-CoA + CO₂ + NADH

    PDC Cofactor Requirements¶

    Cofactor Vitamin Source Role
    TPP (Thiamine pyrophosphate) Vitamin B1 (Thiamine) Decarboxylation of pyruvate
    Lipoic acid Synthesised endogenously; diet Electron/acyl carrier (oxidised/reduced cycle)
    CoA (Coenzyme A) Vitamin B5 (Pantothenate) Acetyl group acceptor
    FAD Vitamin B2 (Riboflavin) Reoxidises dihydrolipoamide (E3 subunit)
    NAD⁺ Vitamin B3 (Niacin) Final electron acceptor → NADH

    Clinical note: B1 (thiamine) deficiency blocks PDC → pyruvate cannot enter Krebs → ATP production fails → Wernicke's encephalopathy, lactic acidosis, cardiac failure.

    Step-by-Step Detail¶

    Step Enzyme Reaction Cofactors Output Regulation
    1 Citrate synthase Oxaloacetate (4C) + Acetyl-CoA (2C) → Citrate (6C) + CoA CoA-SH Citrate Inhibited by ATP, NADH, succinyl-CoA, citrate
    2 Aconitase Citrate ⇌ Isocitrate (via cis-aconitate) Fe-S cluster (non-haem iron) Isocitrate Inhibited by fluorocitrate (aconite toxin)
    3 Isocitrate dehydrogenase Isocitrate → α-Ketoglutarate + CO₂ + NADH NAD⁺, Mn²⁺ or Mg²⁺ NADH, CO₂ Activated by ADP, Ca²⁺; inhibited by ATP, NADH
    4 α-Ketoglutarate (α-KG) dehydrogenase complex α-KG → Succinyl-CoA + CO₂ + NADH TPP (B1), Lipoic acid, CoA (B5), FAD (B2), NAD⁺ (B3) NADH, CO₂ Inhibited by succinyl-CoA, NADH, ATP; activated by Ca²⁺
    5 Succinyl-CoA synthetase Succinyl-CoA → Succinate + GTP Mg²⁺, GDP/ADP + Pᵢ GTP (or ATP) Substrate-level phosphorylation
    6 Succinate dehydrogenase (Complex II) Succinate → Fumarate + FADH₂ FAD (B2), Fe-S clusters, CoQ10 FADH₂ Inhibited by oxaloacetate, malonate
    7 Fumarase (Fumarate hydratase) Fumarate + H₂O → L-Malate None Malate Minor; product inhibition
    8 Malate dehydrogenase L-Malate → Oxaloacetate + NADH NAD⁺ NADH Equilibrium strongly favours malate; pulled by citrate synthase

    📊 Net Yield Per Acetyl-CoA (1 Turn)¶

    Product Quantity Energy contribution
    NADH 3 ~7.5 ATP each via Complex I → ~22.5 ATP
    FADH₂ 1 ~1.5 ATP via Complex II → ~1.5 ATP
    GTP 1 ≈ 1 ATP (directly)
    CO₂ 2 Waste (excreted via respiration)
    Net ATP per turn ~25 ATP (theoretical maximum)

    Per glucose (2 turns): ~20–24 ATP from TCA products (accounting for proton leak and efficiency losses in vivo).

    🧬 Cofactor Dependencies — Full Reference¶

    Cofactor Vitamin / Mineral Steps Required Deficiency Consequence
    NAD⁺ B3 (Niacin) Steps 3, 4, 8 (×3 NADH) ↓ TCA flux → ↓ ATP → Pellagra; lactic acidosis
    FAD B2 (Riboflavin) Step 4 (α-KG DH), Step 6 (SDH) ↓ FADH₂ → ↓ ETC Complex II input
    CoA (Coenzyme A) B5 (Pantothenate) PDC bridge + Step 4 + Step 5 ↓ Acetyl-CoA entry; impaired fatty acid oxidation
    TPP (Thiamine pyrophosphate) B1 (Thiamine) PDC bridge, Step 4 (α-KG DH) Wernicke's, lactic acidosis, ↓ α-KG → ↓ glutamate/GABA
    Lipoic acid Endogenous / diet PDC bridge, Step 4 Impaired PDC and α-KG DH function
    Fe-S clusters (aconitase) Iron (Fe²⁺) Step 2 ↓ Citrate → isocitrate conversion; TCA block
    Mg²⁺ Magnesium Steps 3, 5 (enzyme cofactors) ↓ Isocitrate DH, succinyl-CoA synthetase activity
    Mn²⁺ Manganese Step 3 (Isocitrate DH in some species) Minor; usually Mg²⁺ is primary in humans
    Ca²⁺ Calcium Activation of Steps 3, 4 (mitochondrial) ↓ TCA activation during muscle contraction/signalling
    CoQ10 (Ubiquinone) Endogenous; diet/supplements Step 6 (SDH → ETC handoff) ↓ FADH₂ → ETC transfer; statin-induced CoQ10 depletion

    🔗 Connections to Neurotransmitter Synthesis¶

    The Krebs cycle is directly upstream of several neurotransmitter pathways:

    Krebs Intermediate NT Pathway Connection
    α-Ketoglutarate (α-KG) Transaminated → Glutamate (→ GABA via GAD); major excitatory NT precursor
    Oxaloacetate (OAA) Transaminated → Aspartate (precursor to pyrimidines and asparagine; excitatory NT)
    Succinyl-CoA Required for haem synthesis (haemoglobin, cytochromes, myoglobin)
    Acetyl-CoA (entry point) Also substrate for Acetylcholine synthesis (via ChAT: Acetyl-CoA + Choline → ACh)
    GTP (Step 5) Substrate for BH4 synthesis (GCH1 step) → BH4 is cofactor for TPH (serotonin) and TH (dopamine)
    NADH / NADPH Powers BH4 recycling (DHPR uses NADH; DHFR uses NADPH) 
    Krebs Cycle
    │
    ├── α-KG ──► Glutamate ──► GABA
    │                       └──► Glutamine
    │
    ├── GTP ────► BH4 ──► Serotonin (TPH), Dopamine (TH), NE, EPI
    │
    ├── Acetyl-CoA ──► Acetylcholine (ChAT)
    │
    └── NADH ──► ETC ──► ATP (powers all NT synthesis, vesicle loading, axonal transport)

    🔀 Anaplerosis — Replenishing the Cycle¶

    Since intermediates are constantly drained for biosynthesis (glutamate, aspartate, haem, etc.), they must be replenished. This is called anaplerosis:

    Anaplerotic Reaction Enzyme Source
    Pyruvate + CO₂ → Oxaloacetate Pyruvate carboxylase (requires Biotin/B7) Gluconeogenesis, lactate
    Amino acids → α-KG (e.g. Glutamate → α-KG) Glutamate dehydrogenase, aminotransferases Protein catabolism
    Propionyl-CoA → Succinyl-CoA Methylmalonyl-CoA mutase (requires B12) Odd-chain fatty acids, valine, isoleucine, methionine
    Aspartate → OAA Aspartate aminotransferase Amino acid catabolism
    Fatty acids (even-chain) β-oxidation → Acetyl-CoA Fat catabolism (not strictly anaplerotic)

    Note: B7 (Biotin) deficiency impairs pyruvate carboxylase → OAA cannot be replenished → TCA cycle slows → ↓ gluconeogenesis and ↓ energy. B12 deficiency impairs propionyl-CoA → succinyl-CoA → odd-chain fat catabolism blocked (methylmalonic aciduria).

    🧪 Cataplerosis — Draining Intermediates for Biosynthesis¶

    Intermediate Drained Used For
    Acetyl-CoA Fatty acid synthesis, cholesterol, ketone bodies, Acetylcholine
    α-Ketoglutarate Glutamate, Glutamine, GABA, proline, arginine synthesis
    Oxaloacetate Gluconeogenesis (via PEP carboxykinase), aspartate
    Succinyl-CoA Haem/porphyrin synthesis; ketone body activation
    Citrate Fatty acid synthesis (exported to cytoplasm → ATP citrate lyase → Acetyl-CoA)
    Malate Gluconeogenesis; NADPH production (via malic enzyme)

    🚦 Regulation¶

    The Krebs cycle is tightly regulated by energy charge (ATP:ADP:AMP ratio) and redox state (NADH:NAD⁺):

    Key Regulatory Points¶

    Enzyme Activated By Inhibited By
    Citrate synthase ADP, substrate availability ATP, NADH, succinyl-CoA, citrate
    Isocitrate dehydrogenase ADP, Ca²⁺ (mitochondrial) ATP, NADH
    α-KG dehydrogenase complex Ca²⁺, ADP ATP, NADH, succinyl-CoA
    PDC (upstream bridge) Ca²⁺, insulin (activates PDC phosphatase) ATP, NADH, acetyl-CoA (product inhibition); PDC kinase (phosphorylation inactivates)

    Ca²⁺ as activator: During muscle contraction or neuronal firing, intracellular Ca²⁺ rises → activates isocitrate DH and α-KG DH → accelerates TCA → more ATP produced to meet demand. This is a direct link between neuronal activity and mitochondrial energy production.

    Feedback Summary¶

    High ATP/NADH (energy replete)
    │
    ├── Inhibits Citrate synthase → ↓ cycle entry
    ├── Inhibits Isocitrate DH → ↓ Step 3
    └── Inhibits α-KG DH → ↓ Step 4
          → TCA slows → NADH/ATP production drops → cycle resumes when demand rises

    🚨 Clinical Consequences of Krebs Cycle Disruption¶

    Deficiency / Condition Primary Block Consequence
    Thiamine (B1) deficiency PDC + α-KG DH paralysed Lactic acidosis, ↓ ATP, ↓ glutamate/GABA; Wernicke's encephalopathy (neuronal ATP failure)
    Riboflavin (B2) deficiency ↓ FAD → ↓ α-KG DH + ↓ SDH Reduced TCA throughput; impaired ETC Complex II
    Niacin (B3) deficiency ↓ NAD⁺ → Steps 3, 4, 8 blocked Pellagra (dermatitis, diarrhoea, dementia); mitochondrial failure
    Pantothenate (B5) deficiency ↓ CoA → ↓ Acetyl-CoA entry + Step 4 Burning feet syndrome; ↓ ACh synthesis; global energy failure
    Biotin (B7) deficiency ↓ Pyruvate carboxylase → ↓ OAA anaplerosis Ketoacidosis, ↓ gluconeogenesis, TCA intermediate depletion
    Vitamin B12 deficiency ↓ Methylmalonyl-CoA mutase → propionyl-CoA accumulates Methylmalonic aciduria; odd-chain fatty acid accumulation (neurotoxic); succinyl-CoA depletion
    Iron deficiency ↓ Aconitase (Fe-S clusters) + ↓ haem Impaired isocitrate → α-KG; ↓ cytochrome c → ETC block
    Magnesium deficiency ↓ Isocitrate DH, succinyl-CoA synthetase Reduced TCA flux; impaired ATP synthesis
    Arsenic poisoning Inhibits lipoic acid (blocks PDC + α-KG DH) Severe lactic acidosis; multi-organ failure
    Fluoroacetate (rat poison) Converted to fluorocitrate → inhibits aconitase Citrate accumulation; TCA block; fatal
    SDHB/SDHC/SDHD mutations Succinate dehydrogenase deficiency Hereditary paraganglioma/phaeochromocytoma; succinate accumulates (oncometabolite)
    Mitochondrial disease (mtDNA) ETC complex defects → ↑ NADH:NAD⁺ → TCA product inhibition Lactic acidosis, Leigh syndrome, MELAS

    🧠 Krebs Cycle and the Brain¶

    The brain is almost entirely dependent on oxidative phosphorylation (fuelled by TCA), consuming ~20% of total body oxygen despite being 2% of body weight. Neurons have very limited glycolytic reserve.

    Neurological Connection Mechanism
    Energy for all neurotransmitter synthesis ATP powers amino acid uptake, enzymatic synthesis, vesicle packaging
    Glutamate/GABA homeostasis α-KG ↔ Glutamate ↔ GABA; Krebs intermediates feed this directly
    Acetylcholine synthesis Acetyl-CoA (TCA entry metabolite) is the direct substrate for ChAT
    BH4 production (→ dopamine, serotonin) GTP produced at Step 5 is substrate for GCH1 (BH4 synthesis)
    BH4 recycling NADPH (via malic enzyme from malate) and NADH (DHPR) are TCA-linked
    Nitric oxide (NO) nNOS requires BH4; impaired TCA → ↓ GTP → ↓ BH4 → ↓ NO + NOS uncoupling
    Axonal transport Kinesin/dynein motors require ATP; TCA failure → transport arrest → neurodegeneration
    Calcium signalling Ca²⁺ activates TCA (Steps 3, 4); reciprocally, TCA failure → dysregulated Ca²⁺

    💊 Pharmacological and Supplementation Relevance¶

    Supplement / Drug Mechanism Krebs Relevance
    CoQ10 ETC electron carrier (FADH₂ → Complex III) Supports SDH (Step 6) → ETC handoff; statin-depleted
    Alpha-Lipoic Acid (ALA) Cofactor for PDC + α-KG DH; antioxidant Supports 2 critical TCA entry/flow steps
    B-complex vitamins B1/B2/B3/B5 are direct TCA cofactors Comprehensive Krebs support
    Magnesium glycinate Mg²⁺ for isocitrate DH + succinyl-CoA synthetase Removes rate-limiting factor in many depleted individuals
    N-Acetyl Cysteine (NAC) Antioxidant; protects mitochondrial proteins Prevents oxidative inactivation of Fe-S enzymes (aconitase)
    Creatine Phosphocreatine ↔ ATP buffer; spares TCA demand Reduces acute ATP deficit during TCA insufficiency
    L-Carnitine Fatty acid transport into mitochondria Maximises Acetyl-CoA supply for TCA cycle
    Succinic acid TCA intermediate; directly enters at Step 6 Experimental in mitochondrial disease; bypasses upstream blocks
    Metformin Inhibits Complex I → ↑ NADH:NAD⁺ → TCA slowdown Reduces hepatic gluconeogenesis (OAA diverted less)
    Statins Deplete CoQ10 (via mevalonate pathway) Impairs SDH → ETC electron transfer; myopathy risk

    ⚠️ Signs of Impaired Krebs Cycle Function¶

    Symptom Pattern Likely Block Key Missing Nutrient
    Chronic fatigue + brain fog + lactic acidosis PDC block B1 (Thiamine)
    Fatigue + neuropathy + methylmalonic acid↑ Anaplerosis (propionyl-CoA → succinyl-CoA) B12
    Pellagra (dermatitis, dementia, diarrhoea) NAD⁺ depletion (Steps 3, 4, 8) B3 (Niacin)
    Muscle weakness + myopathy (especially statin users) CoQ10 depletion → SDH/ETC block CoQ10
    Burning feet + fatigue + neurological symptoms CoA depletion B5 (Pantothenate)
    Anaemia + fatigue + iron-deficiency neuropathy Aconitase (Fe-S cluster loss) Iron
    Fatigue + hypertension + insulin resistance Mg²⁺ depletion → multiple TCA enzyme failure Magnesium
    Depression + anxiety + low dopamine AND serotonin ↓ GTP (Step 5) → ↓ BH4 → ↓ TPH + TH Mitochondrial health (B1, B2, B3, CoQ10)

    Summary¶

    The Krebs cycle is the metabolic engine of the cell. It requires B1, B2, B3, B5, B7, B12, Iron, Magnesium, CoQ10, and Lipoic acid as direct cofactors. Its outputs — NADH, FADH₂, and GTP — power the ETC (for ATP), BH4 synthesis (for serotonin and dopamine), and acetylcholine production. Impaired TCA function therefore cascades into energy failure, neurotransmitter depletion, and metabolic disease.
