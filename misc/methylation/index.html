    Methylation: A Comprehensive Overview¶

    Methylation is one of the most fundamental biochemical processes in the body it's essentially the transfer of a methyl group (CH) from one molecule to another. This simple reaction, happening billions of times per second, governs gene expression, neurotransmitter synthesis, detoxification, immune function, and much more.

    The Core Chemistry¶

    A methyl group is just one carbon atom bonded to three hydrogen atoms (CH). Methylation is the act of adding this group to a substrate a DNA base, a protein, a neurotransmitter, a toxin, etc.

    The universal methyl donor in the body is SAM (S-Adenosylmethionine), which is synthesized from methionine (an essential amino acid) and ATP.

    Methionine + ATP  SAM  (donates CH)  SAH (S-Adenosylhomocysteine)
                                                  
                                           Homocysteine
                                                  
                          (via B12/folate/B6)  Methionine (recycled)

    When SAM donates its methyl group, it becomes SAH, then homocysteine. Homocysteine is either: - Re-methylated back to methionine (requires B12 + methylfolate/5-MTHF), or - Transsulfurated to cysteine glutathione (requires B6)

    Elevated homocysteine is a marker of impaired methylation and is associated with cardiovascular disease, neurodegeneration, and inflammation.

    1. DNA Methylation (Epigenetics)¶

    This is the most studied form. DNA methylation silences gene expression without altering the DNA sequence this is epigenetics.

    Mechanism¶

    • A DNA methyltransferase (DNMT) enzyme adds a methyl group to the 5-position of cytosine (5-methylcytosine, or 5mC), almost always at CpG dinucleotides (cytosine next to guanine).
    • Methylated CpG islands in a gene's promoter region gene is silenced.
    • Unmethylated promoters gene is expressed.

    Why It Matters¶

    State Effect
    Hypermethylation of tumor suppressor genes Cancer risk
    Hypomethylation of oncogenes Cancer risk
    Appropriate methylation of imprinted genes Normal development
    Methylation of viral DNA sequences Immune defense
    Loss of methylation patterns Aging, Alzheimer's

    Inheritance¶

    Methylation patterns can be passed to daughter cells during cell division (mitotic inheritance) and in some cases across generations (transgenerational epigenetics).

    2. Neurotransmitter Synthesis & Breakdown¶

    Methylation is critical for brain chemistry:

    Reaction Enzyme Cofactor
    Norepinephrine Epinephrine PNMT SAM
    Serotonin Melatonin ASMT SAM
    Dopamine breakdown COMT SAM, Mg
    Histamine breakdown HNMT SAM

    COMT (Catechol-O-Methyltransferase) is particularly important it methylates and inactivates catecholamines (dopamine, epinephrine, norepinephrine). The common COMT Val158Met SNP slows this enzyme, leading to higher dopamine/norepinephrine levels, which can affect mood, stress response, and pain sensitivity.

    3. Detoxification¶

    The liver uses methylation in Phase II detoxification to neutralize and excrete:

    • Estrogens methylation converts 2-OHE1 (less harmful) to 2-methoxyestrone (excreted). Impaired methylation accumulation of 16-OHE1 (more carcinogenic)
    • Heavy metals arsenic is methylated for urinary excretion
    • Drugs & xenobiotics many medications are methylated for clearance
    • Toxins various environmental chemicals

    4. Phospholipid & Membrane Synthesis¶

    Methylation is required to synthesize phosphatidylcholine (PC), a key component of every cell membrane, via the PEMT pathway:

    Phosphatidylethanolamine  (3 methylation via PEMT + SAM)  Phosphatidylcholine

    This is why choline is so important it spares the methylation demand. Low choline intake increases the methylation burden on SAM.

    5. The Folate Cycle & One-Carbon Metabolism¶

    Methylation doesn't work in isolation it's part of the one-carbon metabolism network, which includes:

    
                  FOLATE CYCLE                           
  Dietary Folate  DHF  THF  5,10-MTHF  5-MTHF      
                                                       
              (via MTHFR enzyme)          Homocysteine   

                                                 

               METHIONINE CYCLE                          
   Homocysteine  (B12 + 5-MTHF)  Methionine  SAM

    Key Enzyme: MTHFR¶

    Methylenetetrahydrofolate reductase converts 5,10-MTHF 5-MTHF (active methylfolate), which then donates its methyl group to convert homocysteine methionine.

    The MTHFR C677T SNP (very common ~40% of people have one copy) reduces enzyme efficiency by 3070%, impairing the folate methylation pathway. These individuals often benefit from 5-MTHF (methylfolate) supplementation rather than folic acid.

    6. Protein Methylation (Histones)¶

    Beyond DNA, methylation occurs on histone proteins (which DNA wraps around):

    • Histone methyltransferases (HMTs) add methyl groups to specific lysine or arginine residues on histone tails
    • Effect depends on which residue is methylated and how many methyl groups:
    • H3K4me3 gene activation
    • H3K27me3 gene repression
    • H3K9me3 heterochromatin formation

    This is another major layer of epigenetic control.

    7. RNA Methylation (Epitranscriptomics)¶

    A newer, rapidly emerging field methylation of RNA:

    • m6A (N6-methyladenosine) the most common RNA modification; regulates mRNA stability, translation, and splicing
    • Written by "writers" (METTL3/14), read by "readers" (YTHDF proteins), erased by "erasers" (FTO, ALKBH5)
    • Dysregulated in cancers, metabolic disease, and neurological conditions

    Key Nutrients for Methylation¶

    Nutrient Role
    Methionine Precursor to SAM
    Folate (5-MTHF) Regenerates methionine from homocysteine
    Vitamin B12 (methylcobalamin) Cofactor for methionine synthase
    Vitamin B6 (P5P) Cofactor for transsulfuration (homocysteine cysteine)
    Riboflavin (B2) Cofactor for MTHFR enzyme
    Choline Spares SAM via the PEMT pathway
    Betaine (TMG) Alternative methyl donor via BHMT enzyme
    Zinc, Magnesium Cofactors for methyltransferase enzymes
    Glycine Buffers excess methyl groups

    Common SNPs Affecting Methylation¶

    SNP Gene Impact
    C677T MTHFR folate methylation conversion
    A1298C MTHFR BH4, affects neurotransmitters
    Val158Met COMT catecholamine breakdown
    A2756G MTR methionine synthase activity
    A66G MTRR B12 recycling

    Consequences of Methylation Dysfunction¶

    Undermethylation (too little): - Depression, OCD, anxiety (low serotonin) - Autoimmune conditions - Elevated homocysteine cardiovascular risk, blood clots, stroke - Impaired detoxification toxin accumulation

    Overmethylation (too much): - Anxiety, paranoia, low motivation - Histamine intolerance - Can suppress genes that should be active

    Clinical Testing¶

    Test What It Measures
    Plasma homocysteine Overall methylation capacity
    MTHFR genotyping Genetic methylation risk
    Whole blood histamine Methylation status (low = overmethylator)
    SAM:SAH ratio Direct methylation adequacy
    Methylmalonic acid Functional B12 status
    Organic acids Folate/B12 functional deficiency

    Summary¶

    Methylation is a master regulator of biology it controls which genes are on or off, how your brain chemicals are balanced, how efficiently you detoxify, and how resilient your cells are. It's not a single pathway but a metabolic network interconnected through the methionine cycle, folate cycle, and transsulfuration pathway. Nutrients (B12, folate, B6, choline), genetics (MTHFR, COMT), and lifestyle (diet, stress, toxins) all profoundly influence methylation capacity.
