    Lycopene¶

    Overview¶

    Lycopene is a bright red carotenoid pigment responsible for the characteristic colour of tomatoes, watermelon, pink grapefruit, and guava. It is an acyclic carotene (no -ionone ring), which distinguishes it structurally from -carotene and renders it unable to be converted to vitamin A making its health effects entirely independent of vitamin A activity. Lycopene is the most potent singlet oxygen quencher among all dietary carotenoids (2 -carotene, 10 -tocopherol), making it an exceptionally effective antioxidant. Lycopene accumulates preferentially in the testes, adrenal glands, and liver. Its most studied clinical association is with prostate cancer risk reduction, supported by epidemiological data, though large intervention trials have been mixed.

    • Classification: Acyclic carotenoid; fat-soluble antioxidant; no vitamin A activity (no -ionone ring)
    • Structure: 40-carbon polyene with 11 conjugated double bonds (record among common carotenoids); highly unsaturated structure confers exceptional singlet oxygen quenching
    • Forms: All-trans-lycopene (predominant in raw tomatoes); cis-lycopene (5-cis, 9-cis, 13-cis) processing increases cis content; cis-lycopene has better bioavailability than trans
    • Key property: Processing (heating, homogenising, pureing) dramatically increases bioavailability of lycopene from tomatoes making tomato paste/sauce more bioavailable than raw tomatoes

    Functions¶

    • Singlet oxygen (O) quenching: Lycopene's conjugated double bond system makes it the most efficient singlet oxygen quencher of dietary carotenoids physically dissipates the excitation energy of O returns to ground state without being consumed; the carotenoid is regenerated (unlike vitamin C or E which are consumed)
    • Peroxyl radical scavenging: Lycopene interrupts lipid peroxidation chain reactions in cell membranes and LDL particles
    • LDL oxidation protection: Lycopene incorporated into LDL reduces its susceptibility to oxidative modification potentially reduces atherogenic ox-LDL formation
    • Cell growth regulation: Inhibits cancer cell proliferation via gap junction communication enhancement (Cx43 connexin upregulation) restores growth-suppressive cell-cell communication disrupted in cancer; independent of antioxidant activity
    • Androgen receptor modulation: Downregulates androgen receptor (AR) signalling in prostate cells potentially reduces prostate cell proliferation driven by androgens
    • IGF-1 pathway inhibition: Lycopene reduces IGF-1 receptor signalling (a growth driver in prostate, breast, and colorectal cancers)
    • Phase II enzyme induction: Activates Nrf2 induces glutathione S-transferase, NQO1, heme oxygenase-1 cellular antioxidant and detoxification capacity enhancement

    Metabolism and Bioavailability¶

    Factor Effect on Bioavailability
    Heating / processing Increases lycopene bioavailability: disrupts plant cell walls, releases lycopene from chromoplasts, isomerises trans cis (more bioavailable); tomato paste/sauce 5 higher plasma response vs raw tomato
    Dietary fat co-ingestion Fat is required: lycopene is incorporated into micelles via bile salts; adding olive oil to tomato sauce dramatically increases lycopene absorption (cook with oil)
    Food matrix Fine particle size (homogenisation, blending) and cooking increase bioaccessibility; whole tomato < blended tomato < tomato paste
    Cholesterol-lowering drugs (cholestyramine, ezetimibe) May impair carotenoid absorption (bile acid sequestrants impair micelle formation)
    Absorption Absorbed in small intestine in micelles incorporated into chylomicrons lymph plasma distributed to tissues
    Plasma distribution LDL (37%), HDL (14%), VLDL (14%), other lipoproteins; plasma lycopene is an excellent biomarker of tomato intake
    Tissue accumulation Testes (~4.3 nmol/g wet tissue), adrenal glands, liver highest tissue concentrations; prostate accumulates significant lycopene
    Half-life ~1233 days in plasma

    Cooking tip: Adding olive oil when making tomato sauce increases lycopene absorption by 23 vs. oil-free preparation. Tomato paste is the most concentrated and bioavailable source. Watermelon provides lycopene with excellent bioavailability (already high cis-lycopene content, high water activity aids absorption).

    Clinical Relevance¶

    Prostate Cancer¶

    • The most extensively studied association: epidemiological data (Harvard NHS cohort, >40,000 men) shows highest lycopene intake associated with ~2135% lower risk of prostate cancer, particularly aggressive/advanced prostate cancer
    • Lycopene accumulates in the prostate; men with prostate cancer have lower prostate lycopene than controls
    • Mechanisms: AR downregulation, IGF-1 inhibition, cell cycle arrest (G/G), gap junction restoration, antioxidant protection of DNA
    • Intervention trials: mixed. Several small RCTs show reduced PSA or improved histological outcomes; SELECT trial (selenium+vitamin E) found no benefit but did not test lycopene; definitive large RCT lacking
    • WCRF/AICR: "Limited, suggestive evidence" for lycopene and prostate cancer cannot make a firm recommendation based on current evidence

    Cardiovascular Disease¶

    • Plasma lycopene is inversely associated with carotid intima-media thickness (cIMT), a marker of subclinical atherosclerosis, in multiple cross-sectional studies
    • Lycopene inhibits HMG-CoA reductase (modestly) may contribute to cholesterol lowering
    • LYRIS trial (2019): 7 mg lycopene/day for 2 months in patients with CVD significantly improved brachial artery flow-mediated dilation (FMD, a marker of endothelial function) vs placebo

    Skin Photoprotection¶

    • Lycopene in skin provides modest UV protection acts as an endogenous sunscreen; higher skin lycopene levels less UV-induced erythema
    • Tomato paste (40 g/day; ~16 mg lycopene) for 10 weeks significantly reduced UV-induced skin erythema vs olive oil control
    • Does not replace topical sunscreen (SPF effect is minor) but complements it

    Fertility (Male)¶

    • Testes have the highest lycopene concentration of any tissue; oxidative stress is a major driver of male infertility
    • Small RCTs suggest lycopene supplementation improves sperm count, motility, and morphology in oligospermic men; a systematic review confirmed modest benefit

    Hypertension¶

    • Meta-analysis: lycopene supplementation (>12 mg/day in studies) modestly reduced systolic BP (~5 mmHg) vs placebo; effect appears threshold-dependent (above ~25 mg/day of dietary intake)

    Interactions¶

    Factor Interaction
    Fat-soluble antioxidants (-carotene, lutein, zeaxanthin) Carotenoids compete for intestinal absorption (shared SR-BI and CD36 transporters); very high supplemental -carotene may reduce lycopene absorption and vice versa
    Cholesterol-lowering drugs Bile acid sequestrants (cholestyramine, colestipol) impair fat-soluble compound absorption including lycopene; ezetimibe (NPC1L1 inhibitor) may reduce carotenoid absorption
    Dietary fat Required for lycopene absorption (micelle formation); advise consumption of lycopene-rich foods with healthy fats (olive oil, avocado)
    Phytosterol supplements May reduce carotenoid absorption at high doses (shared micellar solubilisation pathway)
    Anticoagulants At very high doses, lycopene may have mild anti-platelet effects clinically insignificant at food intake levels
    Finasteride/dutasteride (5-ARIs) Combined with prostate cancer-protective lifestyle including lycopene no drug interaction data, but 5-ARIs and lycopene may complementarily reduce prostate cancer risk

    Supplementation Notes¶

    • Dietary approach preferred: Processed tomato products (paste, sauce, juice) with olive oil are the most effective means of achieving high lycopene bioavailability no need for supplements in most people
    • Supplemental dose: 1030 mg/day trans-lycopene or mixed isomers used in most RCTs; natural extracts (tomato oleoresin containing mixed isomers) may be preferable to pure all-trans-lycopene
    • No RDA/AI established no official dietary reference intake for lycopene in most countries
    • Safety: No UL established; no toxicity at supplemental doses; lycopene does not cause hypervitaminosis A (no provitamin A activity); high intake may cause lycopenodermia (orange skin discolouration) harmless and reversible
    • Important note: Unlike -carotene (which caused harm in smokers in ATBC and CARET trials), lycopene supplementation has not been associated with adverse effects in smokers; the absence of provitamin A activity may account for this safety difference
    • Absorption optimisation: Take with a fat-containing meal; choose cis-lycopene-rich sources (cooked/processed tomatoes) or supplements containing mixed lycopene isomers
