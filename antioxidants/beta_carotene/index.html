    Beta-Carotene¶

    Overview¶

    Beta-carotene is the most well-known provitamin A carotenoid â€” upon absorption, it can be cleaved by intestinal beta-carotene 15,15'-monooxygenase (BCMO1) into two molecules of retinal (vitamin A aldehyde), making it the most abundant dietary source of preformed-equivalent vitamin A globally, particularly in plant-based diets. It is an orange-yellow pigment synthesised by plants, algae, and fungi but not by animals; its characteristic colour reflects 9 conjugated double bonds that can absorb blue-violet light (~450 nm). Beyond its provitamin A role, beta-carotene functions as a membrane antioxidant and lipid-soluble free radical scavenger at normal physiological concentrations. However, two landmark clinical trials (ATBC and CARET) found that high-dose beta-carotene supplementation significantly increased lung cancer risk in smokers â€” a sobering example of how a nutrient's safety profile in food differs radically from pharmacological supplementation.

    • Classification: Carotenoid; provitamin A (most potent dietary provitamin A); symmetric bicyclic carotene
    • Structure: 40-carbon symmetric carotene; two Î²-ionone rings connected by a polyene chain; 9 conjugated double bonds (responsible for orange colour)
    • Key property: 1 molecule Î²-carotene â†’ theoretical maximum 2 molecules retinal (vitamin A); actual yield varies by conversion efficiency, food matrix, individual genetics (BCMO1 polymorphisms)
    • Vitamin A equivalence: 12 Âµg dietary Î²-carotene from food = 1 Âµg retinol activity equivalent (RAE) [WHO]; 6 Âµg = 1 RAE for supplements

    Functions¶

    Provitamin A Activity¶

    • Provides vitamin A for all retinol-dependent functions: rhodopsin synthesis (night vision), retinoic acid signalling (cell differentiation, immune function, embryogenesis), mucosal integrity, immune competence
    • In developing countries where preformed vitamin A is scarce (no liver, eggs, or dairy), beta-carotene from orange/yellow fruits and vegetables is the primary vitamin A source

    Antioxidant Activity¶

    • At physiological concentrations (pOâ‚‚ <150 mmHg), beta-carotene is an effective singlet oxygen quencher and lipid peroxyl radical chain-breaking antioxidant in cell membranes
    • pOâ‚‚-dependent behaviour: At high partial oxygen pressures (e.g., in lung tissue of smokers with supplemental oxygen or high-dose supplementation), beta-carotene can become a pro-oxidant rather than antioxidant â€” generating peroxyl radicals; this is the mechanistic explanation for the ATBC/CARET findings

    Immune Function (via Vitamin A)¶

    • Vitamin A derived from beta-carotene supports:
    • Mucosal barrier integrity (respiratory, GI)
    • T-cell and B-cell differentiation
    • NK cell and neutrophil function
    • IgA production at mucosal surfaces

    Clinical Relevance¶

    Vitamin A Deficiency (Global Burden)¶

    • Vitamin A deficiency (VAD) affects ~190 million preschool children and 19 million pregnant women globally, primarily in sub-Saharan Africa and South/Southeast Asia
    • Night blindness: The earliest clinical sign of VAD; reversible with adequate beta-carotene or retinol
    • Xerophthalmia and corneal blindness: Advanced VAD; irreversible; leading cause of preventable childhood blindness
    • Biofortification: Golden Rice (transgenic rice expressing Î²-carotene in the endosperm) and biofortified orange sweet potato are strategies to increase beta-carotene delivery in VAD-endemic regions

    The ATBC and CARET Trials â€” Landmark Cautionary Findings¶

    [!CAUTION] ATBC trial (1994): 29,133 male Finnish smokers; beta-carotene 20 mg/day for 5â€“8 years â†’ 18% increased lung cancer incidence, 8% increased overall mortality vs placebo. CARET trial (1996): 18,314 smokers/asbestos workers; 30 mg beta-carotene + 25,000 IU retinyl palmitate â†’ 28% increased lung cancer incidence, 17% increased overall mortality â†’ trial stopped early. Neither supplement group showed benefit in any subgroup.

    • Mechanism (proposed): In the oxidant-rich lung environment of smokers, pharmacological beta-carotene generates reactive beta-carotene radicals â†’ propagate lipid peroxidation â†’ carcinogenic arachidonic acid derivatives; also interferes with RA (retinoic acid) signalling â†’ loss of tumour suppression
    • Key lesson: Food-form beta-carotene is safe (associated with lower cancer risk in observational studies); isolated high-dose supplement beta-carotene is harmful in smokers and possibly in heavy exercisers (similar mechanism)

    Beta-Carotene vs. Lutein/Zeaxanthin in AMD¶

    • AREDS1 formula contained 15 mg beta-carotene; AREDS2 replaced it with 10 mg lutein + 2 mg zeaxanthin: non-inferior for AMD protection, superior in smokers (beta-carotene concerns), and no competitive absorption issues for macular carotenoids

    Carotenodermia (Hypercarotenaemia)¶

    • Harmless orange-yellow discolouration of the skin (palms, soles, nasolabial folds) from very high dietary beta-carotene intake (>30 mg beta-carotene/day from food, e.g., carrot juice drinkers); reversible on reducing intake; does not cause hypervitaminosis A (unlike retinol supplementation)

    Supplementation Notes¶

    • From food: safe and beneficial â€” high dietary beta-carotene from fruits and vegetables is consistently associated with lower cancer and CVD risk in epidemiological studies
    • Supplemental beta-carotene:
    • Contraindicated in smokers and asbestos-exposed individuals (ATBC/CARET evidence)
    • Not recommended as an isolated supplement for general population (WCRF, WHO, ACS)
    • Still present in some multivitamins â€” smokers should choose vitamin A as retinyl palmitate instead
    • For vitamin A supplementation: Preformed retinyl palmitate (IU-dosed) is safer and more reliable than beta-carotene for people with BCMO1 polymorphisms, malabsorption, or significant VAD
    • Natural mixed carotenoids (from algae: Dunaliella salina â€” contains alpha-carotene, beta-carotene, lutein, zeaxanthin together): may be safer than pure synthetic beta-carotene due to the antioxidant buffering of the other carotenoids; not definitively proven
    • Note for vegans: Those following strict vegan diets should assess beta-carotene intake and, if BCMO1 poor-converter status is suspected (genetic testing available), consider preformed vitamin A from supplements or fortified foods
