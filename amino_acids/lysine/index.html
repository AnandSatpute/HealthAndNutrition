    Lysine¶

    Overview¶

    Lysine is an essential amino acid and the most commonly limiting amino acid in cereal-grainbased diets. It is critical for collagen synthesis and cross-linking, immune function, and carnitine biosynthesis. Lysine has a positively charged -amino group that participates in key post-translational modifications (acetylation, methylation, ubiquitination) on histone proteins directly linking lysine to epigenetic regulation of gene expression.

    • Classification: Essential (9 essential amino acids)
    • IUPAC abbreviation: Lys / K
    • Side chain: -amino group (pKa ~10.5); positively charged at physiological pH
    • Glucogenic / Ketogenic: Exclusively ketogenic (along with leucine the only two strictly ketogenic essential amino acids; acetyl-CoA and acetoacetate)

    Dietary Requirements (RDA)¶

    Age / Group RDA (mg/kg/day) Approximate daily need
    Infants 06 months ~99 mg/kg/day
    Children 13 years ~62 mg/kg/day ~1,365 mg/day
    Adults 19+ ~38 mg/kg/day ~2,660 mg/day (70 kg adult)
    Pregnant Women Higher ~3,100 mg/day

    No established UL from food sources. Supplemental doses of 13 g/day are used clinically. Very high doses (>15 g/day) have caused GI symptoms and tubulointerstitial nephritis in isolated case reports.

    Functions¶

    • Collagen Synthesis: Lysine residues are hydroxylated by prolyl hydroxylase (requires vitamin C) to form hydroxylysine essential for cross-linking collagen fibrils via lysyl oxidase (requires copper). Without adequate lysine or vitamin C weakened connective tissue, poor wound healing.
    • Carnitine Biosynthesis: Lysine (and methionine) butyrobetaine L-carnitine (requires vitamin C, B6, niacin, iron as cofactors). Carnitine is essential for long-chain fatty acid transport into mitochondria for -oxidation.
    • Calcium Absorption: Lysine increases intestinal calcium absorption and reduces urinary calcium excretion may play a role in bone mineral density maintenance.
    • Antiviral Activity (Herpes simplex): Arginine is required for HSV replication; lysine competes with arginine for intestinal absorption and intracellular uptake lysine supplementation reduces HSV recurrence frequency and severity in clinical trials.
    • Histone Modification: -amino groups of histone lysine residues undergo:
    • Acetylation (by HATs) gene activation
    • Methylation (by HMTs) activation or repression depending on position (H3K4me3 = active; H3K27me3 = repressive)
    • Ubiquitination protein degradation (via proteasome) or signalling
    • Enzyme Structure: Lysine residues form hydrogen bonds and ion pairs central to enzyme active site architecture.
    • Antibody Production: Lysine availability supports immunoglobulin synthesis.

    Metabolism¶

    Step Details
    Absorption Active transport via b, transporter (SLC7A9 / BAT1) and rBAT shared with arginine, ornithine, cystine
    Catabolism (main) Saccharopine pathway (liver): Lys saccharopine aminoadipate semialdehyde -ketoadipate acetyl-CoA
    Alternative pathway Pipecolic acid pathway (active in brain and peroxisomes)
    Carnitine synthesis Lys -N-trimethyllysine 3-hydroxy-trimethyllysine carnitine precursors
    Hydroxylation Prolyl hydroxylase-like enzymes hydroxylysine in collagen

    Food Sources¶

    Food Serving Lysine (mg)
    Beef (lean, cooked) 85g ~2,300 mg
    Chicken breast (cooked) 85g ~2,200 mg
    Tuna (canned) 85g ~2,100 mg
    Eggs 1 large ~455 mg
    Milk (whole) 240 mL ~660 mg
    Parmesan cheese 28g ~780 mg
    Lentils (cooked) 200g ~1,240 mg
    Quinoa (cooked) 185g ~440 mg
    Seitan (wheat gluten) 100g very low lysine-deficient

    Wheat, corn, and most cereal grains are poor in lysine the primary limiting amino acid in plant-based and cereal-dominant diets. Legumes compensate (high lysine, low methionine).

    Clinical Relevance¶

    Lysinuric Protein Intolerance (LPI)¶

    • Defect in the yLAT14F2hc transporter (dibasic amino acid transporter)
    • Impaired intestinal absorption and renal reabsorption of lysine, arginine, ornithine
    • Results in urea cycle disruption (hyperammonaemia), protein intolerance, lung disease (PAP), haematological findings
    • Treatment: low-protein diet, citrulline supplementation to bypass the defect, lysine supplementation (oral)

    Familial Hyperlysinaemia¶

    • Bifunctional enzyme deficiency (aminoadipic semialdehyde synthase AASS); generally benign
    • Elevated plasma and urine lysine; usually an incidental amino acid screen finding

    Herpes Simplex Virus (HSV) Recurrence¶

    • Lysine supplementation (5003,000 mg/day) reduces frequency and severity of HSV outbreaks
    • Mechanism: competes with arginine (required for HSV replication) at shared intestinal and cellular transporters
    • Evidence: meta-analyses support decreased recurrence; effect size is moderate

    Collagen Disorders / Wound Healing¶

    • Lysine deficiency impaired collagen cross-linking delayed healing, fragile connective tissue
    • Relevant in severe malnutrition, elderly with protein-energy malnutrition

    Interactions¶

    Nutrient / Drug Effect
    Vitamin C Required for hydroxylysine formation (hydroxylation of lysine in collagen) and for carnitine synthesis
    Arginine Competitive antagonist high arginine impairs lysine absorption; lysine:arginine ratio is relevant for HSV management
    Copper Lysyl oxidase (copper-dependent) forms collagen and elastin cross-links with hydroxylysine
    B6, B3, Iron Cofactors for carnitine biosynthesis from lysine and methionine
    Calcium Lysine enhances intestinal Ca absorption; potential for co-supplementation in bone health protocols

    Supplementation Notes¶

    • L-Lysine supplements (5003,000 mg/day): Used for HSV recurrence prevention; widely available OTC
    • Combined with Vitamin C: Sometimes co-supplemented for collagen synthesis / wound healing support
    • Athletes: Lysine is rarely limiting if total protein intake is adequate with varied sources (meat, dairy, legumes)
    • Vegan diets: Should ensure adequate lysine through legumes (lentils, chickpeas, soy, tofu) cereal-only diets risk lysine insufficiency
