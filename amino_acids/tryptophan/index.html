    Tryptophan¶

    Overview¶

    Tryptophan is an essential amino acid with the largest and most structurally complex aromatic side chain of any standard amino acid. It is the sole precursor of serotonin (5-HT), melatonin, and niacin (vitamin B3) in humans. Despite being the least abundant essential amino acid in most dietary proteins, it plays an outsized role in mood regulation, sleep, immune function, and cellular energy metabolism. Its metabolism branches into three major pathways: the kynurenine pathway (~95%), serotonin/melatonin pathway (~1â€“2%), and direct protein incorporation.

    • Classification: Essential (9 essential amino acids)
    • IUPAC abbreviation: Trp / W
    • Side chain: Indole ring (benzene + pyrrole fused); most hydrophobic and bulky aromatic side chain
    • Glucogenic / Ketogenic: Both (â†’ acetoacetyl-CoA [ketogenic]; TCA intermediates via kynurenine pathway [glucogenic])

    Clinical Relevance¶

    Carcinoid Syndrome¶

    • Neuroendocrine tumours (carcinoid) produce large amounts of serotonin from Trp â€” up to 70% of Trp is diverted
    • Results in niacin deficiency (pellagra-like) due to Trp diversion away from NADâº synthesis
    • Features: flushing, diarrhoea, bronchospasm, right heart valve disease

    Depression and the Serotonin Hypothesis¶

    • Tryptophan depletion (acute dietary Trp lowering) causes temporary depressive symptoms in recovered depressed individuals â€” evidence for the serotonin-Trp-mood link
    • SSRIs work downstream of Trp (block 5-HT reuptake) â€” Trp availability is the upstream precursor constraint
    • Dietary Trp alone does not reliably improve depression in unselected individuals

    • Classic pellagra from corn-dominant diets (niacin in corn is bound, unavailable; corn is also Trp-poor)

    • Treatment: high-protein diet + nicotinamide supplementation
